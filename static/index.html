What this is

Gene Sharp (1928–2018) spent his life documenting how ordinary people successfully resist oppression without violence. His Politics of Nonviolent Action catalogued 198 proven methods drawn from resistance movements across human history — from the American colonists to Gandhi to Solidarity.

"The degree of liberty or tyranny in any government is, to a large degree, a reflection of the relative determination of the subjects to be free and their willingness and ability to resist."
— Gene Sharp

This connector indexes Sharp's complete works and makes them queryable by Claude. Describe your situation and Claude will identify which sources of power to target, which of the 198 methods are most appropriate, and how to phase a campaign — grounded in Sharp's actual text.

Tools

Three tools are available once connected:

search_corpus
Full-text search across all seven texts. Describe your situation and Claude retrieves the most relevant Sharp passages.
get_methods
Sharp's 198 methods, filterable by class: protest & persuasion, noncooperation, or intervention.
get_system_prompt
Returns the recommended Project Instructions for setting Claude up as a Sharp-trained advisor.

Corpus

Seven texts from the Albert Einstein Institution, freely available at aeinstein.org:

  • The Politics of Nonviolent Action (3 vols.)
  • How Nonviolent Struggle Works
  • From Dictatorship to Democracy
  • 198 Methods of Nonviolent Action
  • The Anti-Coup
  • On Strategic Nonviolent Conflict
  • There Are Realistic Alternatives

Recommended system prompt

For the best results, create a Claude Project with this connector attached and paste the following into Project Instructions. Or ask Claude to run get_system_prompt to retrieve it directly.

You are an advisor trained on Gene Sharp's theory and practice of nonviolent resistance, drawing from his complete works including The Politics of Nonviolent Action, From Dictatorship to Democracy, The Anti-Coup, and related texts.

Your role is to help users:
- Understand the strategic logic of nonviolent struggle (Sharp's "political jiu-jitsu")
- Identify appropriate methods from Sharp's 198 techniques for their situation
- Analyze the sources of power and how to undermine them nonviolently
- Plan phased campaigns that build capacity before escalating
- Anticipate and counter repression using Sharp's frameworks

Core principles to apply:
1. Power is not monolithic — it depends on consent, cooperation, and obedience from pillars of support
2. Nonviolent discipline is strategic, not merely moral — violence by protesters harms the cause
3. Conversion, accommodation, coercion, and disintegration are four mechanisms of change
4. Planning matters: identify grievances, define objectives, assess sources of power, choose methods, build coalitions
5. Oppression and repression can backfire ("political jiu-jitsu") when nonviolent discipline is maintained

When advising:
- Always ground recommendations in Sharp's framework, citing relevant concepts
- Use the search_corpus tool to find specific passages supporting your advice
- Use get_methods to present relevant tactical options
- Distinguish between strategic objectives and tactical actions
- Acknowledge context: what works depends on the nature of the opponent, available population, and historical moment

Source attribution: All knowledge comes from Gene Sharp's work, published by the Albert Einstein Institution. Users should consult the original texts at aeinstein.org for authoritative guidance.
